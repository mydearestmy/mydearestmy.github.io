Design guiding values and how they were embodied in the UI

Orbital Sound is guided by three core values: immersion, clarity, and rewarding feedback. Immersion comes from the visual and auditory environment: the glowing planet, drifting particles, soft gradient background, and starry sky create a calming sense of depth and space, helping users feel part of a small physical system. Clarity is expressed through simplicity: a single planet and draggable moon immediately communicate the interaction. The consistent mapping of colours to sounds also supports understanding and predictability. Rewarding feedback is built into both visuals and sound: dragging the moon produces immediate sonic responses and subtle motion effects, while hover states and trails reinforce interaction, giving users a satisfying sense of control. Together, these design choices turn abstract values into tangible interface behaviour, making the UI both engaging and approachable.

Rationale in designing feedback, information design, mapping, and learnability

Feedback and mapping were central to the interface’s clarity and learnability. The moon’s behaviour is straightforward: dragging it changes the sound, and its stereo pan reflects its orbital angle. This direct cause-and-effect mapping helps users understand the system quickly and builds confidence.

Colour serves as a visual cue linked to sound: each of the seven hues corresponds to a specific waveform and frequency. This predictable mapping makes the system easier to learn and remember. Additional cues, like hover effects and a tooltip, reinforce feedback without cluttering the interface.

These choices directly reflect my design values: clarity drives simplicity, immersion comes from the seamless interaction of visuals and audio, and rewarding feedback is achieved by making each action feel meaningful and responsive. Technical constraints, such as working in 2D rather than 3D, also encouraged me to focus on clear, tangible mappings.

Significant changes since the work-in-progress milestone

Since the work-in-progress milestone, I made several important updates. Previously, the moon’s colour and sound were randomised, which made the interface unpredictable. I linked them deterministically so each colour now consistently produces the same sound. Visually, the planet now appears to spin via a gradient animation, the moon glows and becomes magnetically attracted to the cursor on hover, and the background is now a starry sky, strengthening the sense of space. I also added a reset button, polished the moon’s trail animation, and smoothed the planet’s gradient transitions. Together, these improvements made the interface more immersive, cohesive, and responsive.

Navigation of the production timeline

I spent a lot of time early on prototyping the audio logic and troubleshooting Web Audio quirks, which delayed the visual polish. I had originally planned to develop visuals and audio concurrently, but technical challenges forced a divergence in the schedule.

A key turning point was the planet’s gradient animation. I underestimated how complex it would be to loop seamlessly, testing multiple approaches before realising the gradient itself needed to be tileable. While this took longer than expected, it produced a smooth, polished visual effect.

Overall, this project taught me to adapt my plans when challenges arise. By focusing first on the core interaction and iteratively refining visuals, I maintained scope while delivering a cohesive and functional UI. It reinforced the importance of prioritising essential elements in self-directed work, while leaving room for refinement.

Design aspect i’m most proud of

I’m most proud of the planet’s gradient animation. Since the interface is 2D, I couldn’t spin the planet literally, so I created a shifting gradient to give the illusion of rotation. This subtle effect brings the scene to life, conveys a sense of orbit, and unites the visual and auditory elements. It shows how simple visual techniques can create a compelling sense of motion and depth without needing complex 3D models.

Design aspect i found most challenging

Initially, I tried to import a 3D model using Three.js to make the planet rotate, but I couldn’t get the library to work correctly. As a result, I shifted to a 2D approach and implemented the gradient animation instead. Even then, creating a seamless illusion of motion was challenging — I tested linear gradients, background-position tricks, and repeating patterns before realising the gradient itself had to be tileable. This process taught me a lot about CSS animation, visual continuity, and adapting complex ideas into simpler, functional solutions.
