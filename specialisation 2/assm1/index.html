Interaction Design Analysis – 7 Poems and Liquid Entropy

Both 7 Poems and Liquid Entropy are interactive poetry websites, but they approach interaction design in completely different ways. 7 Poems is quiet and structured, while Liquid Entropy feels more abstract and unpredictable. They both play with how a user navigates, experiences, and interprets poetry, but the mood, tone, and even how control is given (or taken) from the user sets them apart.

Below is a comparison of the two across four key principles: information design, mapping, characterisation, and feedback.

Information Design

7 Poems adopts a minimal and spatial layout — a yellow background dotted with seven titled boxes. Each title represents a poem, and when idle, the design holds silence and stillness. Once hovered, a subtle shadow suggests interactivity, and upon activation, the box animates a poem word by word, accompanied by ambient sound. Information unfolds over time — slow, poetic, deliberate. The focus is on pacing and emotional tone, not efficiency.

In contrast, Liquid Entropy opens with a sensory overload: glitch effects, reactive visuals, and an absence of clear navigation. The interface is ambiguous — the information design asks users to explore rather than read. It avoids hierarchy in favor of immersion. While confusing at first, it aligns with a high-concept audiovisual art experience where legibility is secondary to mood.

This contrast shows two extremes of information delivery: 7 Poems gently guides the reader to focus on a single idea at a time, while Liquid Entropy rewards those who are willing to decode its structure through exploration. One is time-based clarity; the other is aesthetic complexity.

Mapping

In 7 Poems, mapping is direct and literal. Each of the seven boxes corresponds to a specific poem, and clicking a box triggers its unique word-by-word animation and sound. This spatial layout — evenly distributed boxes on a uniform background — creates a clean visual metaphor: each square is a doorway into a self-contained poem. There's no confusion about where to go or what to expect — interaction is calm and linear.

Liquid Entropy, on the other hand, abandons conventional mapping. There are no obvious buttons or navigational cues. Instead, clicking, dragging, or hovering at different points may trigger sound, animation, or transitions — but never predictably. This ambiguity forces the user to "learn" the interface by experimenting. It's disorienting but intentionally so — the site mimics a feeling of entropy through its lack of fixed input-output logic.

The two designs show a philosophical divide. 7 Poems is about clarity and focus — every input has a stable output. Liquid Entropy is about instability, where control feels temporary and earned. This difference affects how users navigate and how much they feel “in control.”

Characterisation

7 Poems uses interaction as a characterising tool. The site feels delicate, intimate, and respectful of the poem format. The slow appearance of words and the soft ambient sounds create a meditative experience. The interface doesn’t want to compete with the poem — it wants to support it. The green color change on click feels like a gentle signal: you’ve paused the moment.

Liquid Entropy, by contrast, has a strong personality that dominates the experience. It’s glitchy, layered, chaotic — the interface itself feels like a living being. It demands attention, constantly moves, and offers no stable resting point. The user is never just a reader — they’re an intruder or participant in a machine-like environment.

Where 7 Poems acts like a quiet room in a gallery, Liquid Entropy is more like an underground club with no signs. Both sites reflect their subject matter — poetry vs digital abstraction — but they also frame the user very differently. One invites contemplation; the other insists on reaction.

Feedback

In 7 Poems, feedback is subtle but immediate. Hovering adds a soft shadow, letting the user know the boxes are interactive. Clicking causes the box to animate, text to appear, and ambient sound to begin. Crucially, stopping the interaction — clicking again — halts both the animation and the sound. The feedback loop is designed to be emotionally sensitive, matching the pace and tone of the poems.

In Liquid Entropy, feedback is more chaotic. It exists — but not always in ways the user expects. Sometimes the background reacts to the mouse. Other times, a sound triggers with no clear cause. The feedback loop is more artistic than functional — it’s about sensation rather than confirmation. There’s a deliberate mismatch between input and response, which can be frustrating but is also part of the conceptual play.

This contrast reveals different priorities: 7 Poems gives users control and reassurance. Liquid Entropy removes control to emphasize disruption. Each approach uses feedback not just as utility, but as a form of expression.

Final Reflection

These two sites demonstrate how interaction design is never neutral — it shapes how users feel, explore, and make meaning. 7 Poems uses clean mapping, subtle feedback, and emotional pacing to support quiet, poetic reflection. Liquid Entropy distorts those norms, offering a fractured, unpredictable journey that leans into discomfort and abstraction. Both are carefully crafted — and their choices reflect not only aesthetic goals but values about what interaction should do.
